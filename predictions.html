How it works

Every team has a rating based on how well they've been playing. We use these ratings to work out the chance of either team winning any match, with a boost for the three host nations (USA, Canada, Mexico) when they play. Then we play out the whole tournament 20,000 times on a computer and count how often each team reaches each stage.

What to know

The route from the group stage into the last-32 follows the real World Cup rules. A few details of exactly which third-placed teams meet which group winners are simplified — close, but not pinpoint-exact for every combination. Results already played are built in and update each team's rating.

Reading the table

Rank is our world ranking (#1 = most likely to win it all). FIFA is the official FIFA World Ranking from just before the tournament — a different system, shown for comparison. Rating is our own current rating, adjusted for tournament performance — the number and arrow next to it show how much it's moved from before the tournament (hover for the exact pre-tournament value). 1st/2nd are the chance of finishing top or second in the group. Last 32, Last 16, Quarters, Semis, Final and Champions are the chance of reaching that stage — Champions means winning the whole thing.

How the numbers are made. Each team has a rating that goes up after good results and down after bad ones. When two teams meet, the bigger the gap between their ratings, the more likely the stronger team is to win — but upsets and draws can still happen, and the model allows for that. The three host nations get a boost when playing at home, which is strongest in their first group match and gradually tapers off through the rest of the group stage and into the knockout rounds.

For knockout games that end level, we treat it as roughly a coin toss for who goes through on penalties, with a small lean towards the higher-rated team.

This is our own model, run for fun — not an official FIFA forecast, and not betting advice.
